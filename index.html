Abstract

In the field of biomedical engineering, prosthetics or artificial limbs allow amputees to regain function resulting 
from the loss or absence of a limb. Prosthetic joint infections (PJI) have increased in frequency even though the 
infection risk after primary hip and knee arthroplasties is less than 2%. (Bernard et al., 2010). This is because 
more patients are having surgery. The hip and knee joints are the most often replaced joints. Rheumatoid arthritis, 
osteoarthritis, and other degenerative and inflammatory diseases, as well as traumatic joint injuries, are the three 
main causes of joint replacement. The piano and PSAT study are both examples of how antibiotic therapy can help in the 
management of prosthetic joint infection. (Davis et al., 2022). The percentage of infections that were polymicrobial 
differed significantly between presentation types in the results of the PIANO study, proving that the type of infection 
and the timing of the presentation in relation to the original implant date should be used to determine which antibiotic 
is best. (Davis et al., 2022). Blood culture bottles are a new method of detecting infection in prosthetic joints which
is more accurate and less time-consuming.

Introduction

 
Prosthetic limbs are classified according to the level of amputation: above the knee, below the knee, above the elbow, 
and below the elbow. (Lenz, 2021). For example, when performing a total knee arthroplasty, the surgeon will cut down
the front of the patients’ knee, allowing the kneecap to move to move to the side so they can get to the knee joint 
behind it. (Lenz, 2021). The damaged ends of the patient’s shin bone and thigh bone are cut away, allowing new parts 
which are usually made of plastic and metal to be fitted over the ends of both bones creating the new joint. (Lenz, 2021).

Studies on the effect of antibiotic therapy in prosthetic joint infections

The results of the Prosthetic Joint infection in Australia and New Zealand Observational (PIANO) study collected data from 27 
hospitals and 738 cases of prosthetic joint infections (PJI). The study was used to determine how effective different empiric 
intravenous antibiotic regimens would be best suited to cure PJI according to duration of symptoms after the initial prosthesis 
placement. (Davis et al., 2022) An empiric antimicrobial regimen is used before the organism causing the presence of an infection
is known whereas a definitive regimen is instituted where the causative organism is known. (McGraw Hill Medical, 2023). The patients
in the study were put into categories shown in this figure below.      

Another study was performed consisting of 26 patients with prosthetic joint infection following total knee arthroplasty with the
aim of the study to describe the effectiveness of antibiotic suppression therapy (PSAT)(Sandiford et al., 2019). Antibiotic suppression
treatment refers to the long-term, sometimes lifetime use of antibiotics to prevent joint-related sepsis episodes, alleviate symptoms, 
and delay or avoid the need for further surgery (Sandiford et al., 2019). Patients with PJI were given 6 weeks of intravenous (IV) 
antibiotic therapy following surgery. After they were given 6 weeks oral antibiotic therapy. This determined that PSAT would be defined 
as an oral antibiotic therapy occurring after the 12 weeks as a lifelong treatment (Sandiford et al., 2019). The main goal of the study 
was to document the PSAT success rate, which was defined as the absence of sepsis-related hospital admissions, surgical progression, or
mortality from associated reasons. The second goal of the study was to measure the complication rates of PSAT which included any adverse 
reactions to the antibiotic used (Sandiford et al., 2019).

Results

The results from the PIANO cohort revealed that in all three presentation types, early post-operative, late acute and chronic, 
Staphylococcusaureus was the most prevalent pathogen. (Davis et al., 2022). The percentage of infections that were polymicrobial 
differed significantly between presentation types. Polymicrobial infections are caused by more than one type of microbe and may be 
complex, synergistic, or concurrent. (Brogden, 2003)      

In this investigation, PSAT was successfully discontinued in both individuals after one and one and a half years; the infections were 
related to Staph. aureus (1 polymicrobial, 1 MSSA). At the time of the last follow-up, the patients in two cases in which no organisms 
could be found (no growth) were still symptom-free.
