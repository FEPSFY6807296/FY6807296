Abstract

Prosthetic joint infections (PJI) have increased in frequency even though the infection risk after primary hip and knee arthroplasties is less than 2%. (Bernard et al., 2010). This is because more patients are having surgery. The hip and knee joints are the most often replaced joints. Rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis, and other degenerative and inflammatory diseases, as well as traumatic joint injuries, are the three main causes of joint replacement. The piano and PSAT study are both examples of how antibiotic therapy can help in the management of prosthetic joint infection. (Davis et al., 2022). The percentage of infections that were polymicrobial differed significantly between presentation types in the results of the PIANO study, proving that the type of infection and the timing of the presentation in relation to the original implant date should be used to determine which antibiotic is best. (Davis et al., 2022).
